# Literature Review Scaffold

## 1. Passive Investing and Index Construction

This section should review literature on passive investing, benchmark construction, and the economic importance of index methodology. The central point is that an index is not merely a list of securities; it is an allocation rule. Market-capitalisation weighting can lead to concentration when a small number of firms dominate market value.

Potential themes:

- Growth of index funds and ETFs.
- Benchmark design and investability.
- Differences between constituent count and economic diversification.
- Market-cap weighting versus equal weighting.

## 2. Concentration Measurement in Financial Markets

This section should introduce concentration metrics used in finance and industrial organisation.

Relevant metrics:

- Herfindahl-Hirschman Index.
- Top-N concentration ratios.
- Effective number of stocks.
- Entropy-based measures.
- Sector concentration measures.

Key argument:

A 50-stock index and a 500-stock index may have very different nominal breadth, but the effective number of stocks can be far smaller when weights are unevenly distributed.

## 3. Index Concentration and Investor Risk

This section should connect concentration to investor outcomes.

Potential risk channels:

- stock-specific exposure inside broad indices.
- sector-specific exposure inside market benchmarks.
- volatility and drawdown amplification.
- passive investor misperception of diversification.
- correlation between dominant constituents and index-level risk.

## 5. Research Gap

Existing discussions often focus on index returns, fund performance, or ETF growth. Less attention is given to whether Indian equity indices are economically concentrated despite appearing diversified by constituent count.

This paper addresses that gap by constructing a transparent concentration dataset and distinguishing between:

- full-constituent concentration,
- top-10-only concentration,
- sector-level concentration.
