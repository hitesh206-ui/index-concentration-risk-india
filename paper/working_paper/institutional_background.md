# Institutional Background Draft

## Indian Equity Index Ecosystem

Indian equity benchmarks are central to investment management, passive fund construction, derivatives trading, structured products, and performance measurement. The two most visible broad-market benchmarks are the Nifty 50 and the BSE Sensex. Broader market indices such as the Nifty 100 and Nifty 500 are often used to represent larger segments of the Indian listed equity universe. Sectoral indices such as Nifty Bank, Nifty IT, Nifty FMCG, and Nifty Auto provide focused exposure to specific industries.

Most major Indian equity indices are market-capitalisation weighted or float-adjusted market-capitalisation weighted. Under this structure, stocks with higher free-float market capitalisation receive higher index weights. This design is transparent and investable, but it can also create concentration when a few large companies dominate the underlying market capitalisation.

## Passive Investing and Benchmark Risk

As passive products grow, index composition becomes more economically important. A passive investor may believe that an index containing 50, 100, or 500 stocks provides broad diversification. However, if a small number of stocks or sectors account for a large share of index weight, the investor's actual exposure may be more concentrated than the headline number of constituents suggests.

This distinction is especially important for broad indices. A broad index may appear diversified by name and constituent count, while still having substantial exposure to the largest financial, technology, energy, or consumer stocks. Sectoral indices, by contrast, are concentrated by design and should be analysed separately from broad-market indices.

## Index Governance and SEBI Context

Index governance matters because benchmarks influence fund flows, investor behaviour, product design, and risk management. The SEBI Significant Indices framework reflects the regulatory importance of major benchmarks. This project does not evaluate legal compliance. Instead, it asks whether concentration-based metrics can complement existing index-significance frameworks by highlighting risk exposures that may not be visible from index name or constituent count alone.

## Data Availability and Public Transparency

A practical challenge in studying index concentration is public data availability. Some public factsheets provide only top-10 constituent weights rather than full constituent weights. This project therefore distinguishes between full-constituent snapshots, top-10-only snapshots, and sector-level snapshots. This distinction is important for research integrity and prevents partial concentration measures from being misinterpreted as complete index concentration measures.
